After the [Editing phase](editing) has ended, the argument tree is frozen and the Rating phase begins.

Participants now rate arguments by **staking [vote tokens](tokens) on [rating markets](rating-markets)**: staking on an argument's *pro* side says the argument is **underrated**, staking on its *con* side says it is **overrated**. A stake is a correction of the current rating, not a statement of agreement — you profit if the rating corrects your way once the debate ends.

Rating through markets — rather than by simply counting votes — has two effects:

- **Conviction is priced.** The market is a constant-product pair: the more one side is bought, the more expensive further shares of that side become, so strong claims require strong commitment and later stakers take the crowd's earlier judgement into account.
- **Every token staked is also weight.** The total stake an argument attracts determines how heavily it counts against its siblings at [tallying](tallying) — arguments the crowd cares about matter more than arguments nobody rated.

Participants can spread their budget across many arguments or concentrate it on few; since everyone starts with the same budget, no participant's judgement outweighs another's by wealth — only by how well they spend it.
